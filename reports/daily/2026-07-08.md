- Sources: [arXiv:2607.02770](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02770)

### SHiPPO: Recurrent Memory with Transported Polynomial Projections

- Why it matters: Recurrent memory design is central to long-context and agent applications. [SHiPPO](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.03055) addresses a known limitation of fixed-coordinate HiPPO projection memories: they cannot recover order-sensitive changes to already-written memory content. By transporting the approximation family and channel metric along a moving frame via Sylvester dynamics, SHiPPO preserves the online-memory operator while adding right-action transport, all maintainable in a selective-SSM execution environment.
- Implementation method: For any fixed or learnable right-transport path, SHiPPO transports the approximation family and channel metric together. Conditional on that path, the state is ordinary HiPPO in a tied moving frame with Sylvester coefficient dynamics. The authors derive a restricted group-local realization with controller-compatible right actions, exponential-adjusted updates, exact block-affine scan, and recurrent decoding. A simultaneous-reducibility criterion identifies when right transports collapse to static mixing.
- Experimental setup: Controlled diagnostics on prediction error; a finite-field associative-recall diagnostic with interleaved bindings, operations, and queries; ablation that removes the transport pathway.
- Experimental results: Larger current-token write rank improves ordinary prediction error but cannot recover order-sensitive changes to already-written memory. Transported-memory variants recover this signal, which disappears when the transport pathway is removed. Associative-recall diagnostics provide complementary autoregressive evidence.
- Why it passed the filter: Strong theoretical foundation, clean mechanistic diagnostics. Caveat: evaluated on controlled diagnostics rather than broad sequence-modeling benchmarks. The paper provides evidence focused on memory mechanisms, not competitive leaderboard runs.
- Sources: [arXiv:2607.03055](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.03055)

### Variable Bit-width Quantization: Learning Per-Group Precision for "Bigger-but-Smaller" Language Models

- Why it matters: [VBQ](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02893) reframes LLM precision as a learnable, non-uniform resource --- each group of 64 weights learns its own resolution from {1,2,4,8} bits. The result is a consistently heterogeneous allocation pattern stable enough to freeze into a fixed recipe.
- Implementation method: Training-time Gumbel-Softmax relaxation over per-group bit-width {1,2,4,8}, alternating optimization giving precision logits a clean gradient signal. The discovered recipe is frozen and reused without further search.
- Experimental setup: TinyStories (131M), FineWeb-Edu (1.46B), and Apple silicon inference benchmarks. Distributional analysis using KL divergence and argmax-flip rate.
- Experimental results: 131M at 1.82 mean bits achieves PPL 4.2 on TinyStories (vs 55M FP16 PPL 4.4) at 3.8x less storage. 1.46B model on FineWeb-Edu matches a 593M FP16 control at ~3.7x less storage with 2.5x more parameters. The recipe maps to packed low-bit storage for inference: 1.9x decode speedup at 1.0B, 4.7x at 9B on Apple silicon. 69% of groups collapse to 1 bit; the LM head averages 1.09 bits; the first MLP block keeps ~2.5 bits. Deeper layers progressively self-heal quantization error.
- Why it passed the filter: Excellent method substance, clear evaluation, compelling efficiency results, and robustness analysis. Caveat: scaling the search beyond 1.5B parameters remains open.
- Sources: [arXiv:2607.02893](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02893)

### OmniFocus: Query-Guided Modality-Balanced Token Compression for Omni-Modal LLMs

- Why it matters: Omni-modal LLMs process audio and video jointly but generate massive token sequences. [OmniFocus](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.03050) is a training-free query-guided compression method that performs independent importance estimation for each modality, avoiding the bias from unimodal-guided compression.
- Implementation method: Training-free token importance estimation per modality (video and audio), enabling modality-symmetric compression that preserves modality-specific salient evidence while maintaining audio-visual alignment.
- Experimental setup: Qwen2.5-Omni model family across four audio-visual benchmarks at various token retention ratios.
- Experimental results: At 25% token retention, OmniFocus maintains 59.40 accuracy on DailyOmni with Qwen2.5-Omni-7B while delivering up to 1.38x prefill speedup. Outperforms existing baselines on several major benchmark scores at 25% retention.
- Why it passed the filter: Practical efficiency contribution with concrete speedup numbers and multi-benchmark evaluation. Training-free is a plus for deployment.
- Sources: [arXiv:2607.03050](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.03050)

### LACE-SVD: Loss-Aware SVD with Cumulative Error Correction for LLM Compression

- Why it matters: Low-rank LLM compression via SVD typically allocates rank budgets without considering layer-wise loss sensitivity or error propagation through the residual stream. [LACE-SVD](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.03057) addresses both issues.
- Implementation method: Estimates calibration NLL increase from candidate layer-wise compression ratios, solves a budget-constrained allocation problem, then refines the compressed model with closed-form local updates and propagation-aware correction for residual-stream output modules.
- Experimental setup: LLaMA-7B at compression ratio 0.6, compared against Dobi-SVD.
- Experimental results: WikiText-2 PPL of 32.57 on LLaMA-7B versus 46.18 for Dobi-SVD at compression ratio 0.6, a significant improvement.
- Why it passed the filter: Concrete method contribution with a well-motivated problem (error propagation) and strong quantitative improvement over a direct baseline. Caveat: single model evaluation.
- Sources: [arXiv:2607.03057](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.03057)

### Safe Inference-Time Alignment via Lagrangian Reward Augmentation (LARA)

- Why it matters: Inference-time alignment avoids expensive weight updates but typically handles safety constraints through ad-hoc penalty tuning. [LARA](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02781) dualizes the constrained reward-cost objective into a one-dimensional convex problem, producing a principled augmented reward.
- Implementation method: KL-regularized constrained optimization with reward and cost models, dualized to a one-dimensional convex problem over a nonnegative dual variable. The calibrated dual variable defines an augmented reward usable as a drop-in scoring signal in existing inference-time methods.
- Experimental setup: Evaluated on both sequence-level (Best-of-N reranking) and token-level reward-guided decoding methods; compared against finetuning-based direct alignment baselines.
- Experimental results: LARA improves the helpfulness-harmlessness tradeoff. Best-of-N with LARA approaches finetuning-based direct alignment performance. For token-level decoding, the same construction yields a principled dual-calibrated heuristic rather than an exact constrained-policy guarantee.
- Why it passed the filter: Clean theoretical formulation and practical drop-in design. Caveat: exact guarantee applies only to sequence-level methods; token-level use is a heuristic.
- Sources: [arXiv:2607.02781](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02781)

### ACPO: Adaptive Credit Policy Optimization via Fine-Grained Surrogate Entropy

- Why it matters: RL for reasoning LLMs needs better token-level credit assignment than uniform trajectory-level rewards. [ACPO](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.03126) uses a mode-local surrogate entropy to asymmetrically emphasize uncertain decisions in successful rollouts and overconfident tokens in failed rollouts.
- Implementation method: Token-level credit assignment via mode-local surrogate entropy that admits deterministic entropy bounds. Under modal alignment and proximal updates, preserves the policy-gradient direction to leading order.
- Experimental setup: Mathematical reasoning (AIME 2025) and coding (HumanEvalPro) benchmarks; compared against DAPO, GTPO, and SAPO baselines.
- Experimental results: Consistently improves over strong RL baselines including DAPO, GTPO, and SAPO on AIME 2025 and HumanEvalPro.
- Why it passed the filter: Strong RL method with compelling reasoning and coding results. Good baselines. The surrogate entropy approach is novel.
- Sources: [arXiv:2607.03126](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.03126)

### SelfMem: Self-Optimizing Memory for AI Agents

- Why it matters: Existing agent memory frameworks rely on fixed storage, retrieval, and summarization --- rigid across tasks. [SelfMem](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.03726) lets agents explore, evaluate, and refine their own memory strategy using feedback signals, following a "teach to fish" principle.
- Implementation method: Framework provides memory tools and environment with feedback signals; agents explore memory strategies through trial and error guided by the feedback. Model-guided strategy refinement further improves performance.
- Experimental setup: BEAM benchmark at 100K, 500K, and 1M token conversation scales; compared against retrieval, compression, and agent-memory baselines.
- Experimental results: Outperforms strongest baseline official score by 48.7% at 100K, 40.8% at 500K, and 41.9% at 1M. Robust across diverse memory demands.
- Why it passed the filter: Large margin improvements across all conversation scales, a well-motivated self-optimization approach, and multi-scale evaluation. Important for the agent memory problem.
- Sources: [arXiv:2607.03726](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.03726)

### SwarmResearch: Orchestrating Coding Agents for Open-Ended Discovery

- Why it matters: Long-running coding agents tend to converge on a single approach and miss better alternatives. [SwarmResearch](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02807) introduces an orchestrator-subagent design where a Shepherd Agent steers a population of Search Agents operating in parallel git branches.
- Implementation method: Orchestrator-subagent harness: a Shepherd Agent uses global context to steer Search Agents, each with local context in their own git branch. Agents explore in parallel and the orchestrator adapts parallelism at different search depths.
- Experimental setup: Open-ended optimization tasks (13/15 tasks); compared against state-of-the-art LLM-guided evolution and multi-agent techniques, and fixed scaling of serial/parallel agents.
- Experimental results: Discovers better or comparable solutions on 13/15 tasks, driven by higher-level exploration. Orchestrator-guided scaling outperforms fixed scaling.
- Why it passed the filter: Practical multi-agent architecture with strong empirical results across a range of optimization tasks. Caveat: evaluation is on optimization, not general software engineering.
- Sources: [arXiv:2607.02807](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02807)

### Reward Granularity in RLVR: Comparing Process and Outcome Reward Structures for Mathematical Reasoning in Small Language Models

- Why it matters: RLVR typically uses outcome-based rewards only. [This paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02869) provides a systematic comparison of process vs outcome rewards in small models (0.5B), showing that reward granularity is a first-order design decision.
- Implementation method: Qwen2.5-0.5B fine-tuned with GRPO on GSM8K across five reward conditions: no-RL baseline, process-only, outcome-only, and three hybrid weightings.
- Experimental setup: GSM8K test accuracy; GPT-4o error analysis judging step validity, derivation errors, and structural consistency.
- Experimental results: Process-only achieves 63.73% test accuracy versus 53.75% for outcome-only (nearly 10-point gap), with higher step validity and lower deviation from ground-truth chain length. Hybrid reward with low-process/high-outcome underperforms pure outcome, suggesting conflicting optimization signals.
- Why it passed the filter: Clean experimental design, important practical finding for RLVR practitioners, and careful error analysis. Particularly relevant for small models.
- Sources: [arXiv:2607.02869](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02869)

## Papers to watch

### BDLM Mamba-Hybrid: Training Hybrid Block Diffusion Language Models with Partial Bidirectionality

- Why it is interesting: [BDLM Mamba-Hybrid](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02805) addresses a key technical challenge in hybrid diffusion language models --- the reverse Mamba scan that must process the entire sequence. By restricting the reverse scan to the active denoising block, it enables exact caching across blocks.
- Method and results: At 87M parameters, BDLM Mamba-H achieves best perplexity vs attention and full-sequence baselines on C4-en validation. At 350M, it remains competitive. For long-context, it reaches 19.7x throughput of full-sequence DiffuMamba-H at 65K tokens and 3.7x of BDLM attention at 262K.
- Why watch instead of select: Interesting architectural innovation, but at small model scales. The throughput results are promising but need validation at production scale.
- Sources: [arXiv:2607.02805](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02805)

### Reading Between the Dots: Decoding Hidden Computation across Filler Tokens

- Why it is interesting: Frontier LLMs can reason over content-free filler tokens (dots, counting sequences). [This paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.03502) shows that this hidden computation is directly readable from the residual stream --- attention routes the question through filler to answer, logit-lens reveals early fact retrieval, and KV-cache transplants at filler positions causally swap outputs.
- Method and results: Unsupervised decoding pipeline recovers intermediate values with 80-95% accuracy across DeepSeek V3 and Kimi K2 on four task families. Hidden computation that defeats behavioral CoT monitoring is readable from the full computational trace.
- Why watch instead of select: Important AI safety/reliability implications, but the tasks are narrow diagnostic tasks rather than general reasoning evaluation.
- Sources: [arXiv:2607.03502](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.03502)

### APeB: Benchmarking Personalization Ability of LLM Agents

- Why it is interesting: [APeB](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.03162) introduces a benchmark for personalized product search under raw, underspecified queries with diverse user histories. Models handle explicit queries well but struggle with early-stage queries requiring intent and preference discovery.
- Method and results: Built from action logs pairing underspecified intents with rich histories and candidate items. Rubric analysis attributes the performance gap mainly to ineffective history use. A simple VQRA pipeline yields consistent gains.
- Why watch instead of select: Useful benchmark contribution for an underexplored problem (agent personalization under raw queries), but the current setup is limited to product search.
- Sources: [arXiv:2607.03162](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.03162)

### Oyster-II: Reinforcement Learning for Constructive Safety Alignment

- Why it is interesting: [Oyster-II](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02914) moves beyond blanket refusal toward constructive safety alignment, using RL (Zero-RL + multi-stage) to address Oyster-I's SFT limitations: insufficient safety generalization and safety chain-of-thought over-generalization.
- Method and results: Evaluated across extensive safety benchmarks. Claimed to surpass Qwen3-14B and Oyster-I on safety, achieving cross-scale performance comparable to Qwen3-Max and Qwen3.5-397B.
- Why watch instead of select: The cross-scale comparison (14B vs Max/397B) is notable but the paper needs independent validation. The constructive safety paradigm is worth monitoring.
- Sources: [arXiv:2607.02914](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02914)
