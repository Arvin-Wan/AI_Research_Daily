## Selected research papers

### Reliability Scaling Laws for Quantized Large Language Models

- **Why it matters**: Quantization is the standard path to efficiency, but reliability --- how well a model holds up under perturbed or adversarial inputs --- has been completely unexplored for quantized models. This paper fills that gap with the first comprehensive evaluation, and the answer is: quantization reliability scales differently from accuracy. Deployment decisions that optimize for accuracy may leave reliability gaps.
- **Implementation method**: Systematic evaluation across multiple bitwidths and model scales. Introduces Quantization Reliability Ratio (QRR), measuring reliability degradation relative to the full-precision baseline. Evaluates both standard predictive metrics and reliability metrics on perturbed inputs.
- **Experimental results**: Reliability is monotonic with bitwidth but the shape of the curve differs from accuracy. The largest per-bit reliability improvement occurs at 4→5 bits --- not at lower or higher transitions. Reliability does not follow accuracy scaling laws: a model with good accuracy at 4 bits may still have poor reliability.
- **Why it passed the filter**: First-mover advantage on an important practical question. Quantitative methodology. Directly actionable for anyone deploying quantized LLMs in production.
- **Sources**: [arXiv:2607.10855](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.10855)

### Capabilities of Claude Fable 5 on Biomedical Challenge Problems

- **Why it matters**: Biomedical benchmarks are a useful lens for model capability, but most evaluations are undermined by two problems: saturated legacy benchmarks and LLM-as-judge grading. This paper avoids both by using challenging benchmarks and deterministic scoring against fixed answer keys. Claude Fable 5 is Anthropic's current frontier public model, making this a useful capability snapshot.
- **Implementation method**: Evaluation across 8 biomedical benchmarks (4 text: MedQA, MedMCQA, PubMedQA, BioASQ; 4 multimodal: including pathology and radiology tasks). Deterministic scoring against fixed answer keys. Baselines include two Claude predecessors and GPT-5.
- **Experimental results**: Performance varies by domain. Deterministic scoring avoids the instability introduced by LLM-as-judge grading. The paper provides a clean capability snapshot for both Claude Fable 5 and GPT-5 on biomedical tasks.
- **Why it passed the filter**: Methodological rigor (deterministic scoring). Direct comparison between Anthropic and OpenAI frontier models on a well-defined domain. Useful as a capability benchmark regardless of which model wins.
- **Sources**: [arXiv:2607.10849](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.10849)

### Predictive Divergence Masks for LLM Reinforcement Learning

- **Why it matters**: RL for LLMs relies on trust-region mechanisms to stabilize off-policy updates, but the dominant PPO-style approach uses the sampled-token importance ratio for both proximity and direction criteria. DPPO (recent work) fixed the proximity criterion. This paper fixes the direction criterion --- the remaining unsolved piece --- by using probability-based predictive divergence.
- **Implementation method**: Replaces the ratio-based direction test with a predictive-divergence mask. The mask uses the behavior policy's own probability distribution to determine whether an update would push the policy further from the behavior distribution, rather than relying on the sampled token's importance ratio.
- **Experimental results**: Better stabilization of off-policy updates compared to both PPO and DPPO baselines. The direction criterion improvement is the primary contribution.
- **Why it passed the filter**: Solving the remaining piece of an active research direction. Clean conceptual advance. Directly applicable to anyone training LLMs with RL.
- **Sources**: [arXiv:2607.10848](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.10848)

### GRADE: Gated Routing and Adaptive Depth for Efficient Multi-Agent Reasoning

- **Why it matters**: Multi-agent ensembling typically multiplies active parameters without addressing three basic questions: which agents to consult, how deeply to traverse the hierarchy, and when inter-agent communication is worth its cost. GRADE answers all three with learned gates.
- **Implementation method**: Hierarchical multi-agent system with four lightweight learned gates: (1) agent selection, (2) hierarchy depth, (3) inter-agent communication, (4) branch pruning. Trained jointly. The gates are cheap to evaluate at inference time.
- **Experimental results**: Reduces active parameters by 60-90% while maintaining or improving reasoning performance compared to full multi-agent ensembles. The communication gate is especially effective at eliminating unnecessary inter-agent messages.
- **Why it passed the filter**: Practical efficiency gains for multi-agent systems. The gate-learning approach is generalizable. Addresses the core cost problem of agent ensembling.
- **Sources**: [arXiv:2607.10836](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.10836)


### Diagnosing and Mitigating Thinking Collapse in On-Policy Self-Distillation

- **Why it matters**: On-Policy Self-Distillation is a widely used paradigm for aligning LLMs, but in complex reasoning tasks it paradoxically degrades performance. This paper identifies why: a phenomenon called Thinking Collapse --- the model's native intermediate reasoning behavior collapses during training.
- **Implementation method**: Systematic investigation of OPSD degradation in reasoning tasks. Introduces epistemic-token density (ET per 1k) as a quantitative metric for intermediate reasoning richness. Entropy-based analysis reveals the mechanism: the student model learns to shortcut reasoning chains, losing the epistemic tokens that encode intermediate reasoning steps.
- **Experimental results**: Epistemic-token density drops sharply during OPSD for complex reasoning tasks. The drop correlates with downstream performance degradation. Proposed mitigation reduces but does not fully eliminate the collapse.
- **Why it passed the filter**: Diagnoses a real failure mode in a widely used technique. The epistemic-token density metric is a useful diagnostic tool. OPSD practitioners need to know about this pitfall.
- **Sources**: [arXiv:2607.10805](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.10805)

### QIMG-7: Trust Before Fusion --- Source-Aware Resolution for Polluted Multimodal RAG

- **Why it matters**: Multimodal RAG is increasingly deployed in production, but existing evaluations assume clean retrieved evidence. Real retrieval returns corrupted content. QIMG-7 shows that multimodal RAG systems are extremely vulnerable --- and the corruption vectors (entity swaps, typographic overlays, adversarial patches) are diverse and hard to detect.
- **Implementation method**: Controlled benchmark covering 4 datasets, 7 image-attack families, and 16 clean-corrupt pair scenarios. Attack families include: corrupted metadata, entity swaps, typographic overlays, semantic edits, adversarial patches, image blends, and style transfer. Proposes source-aware resolution that assesses evidence reliability before fusion.
- **Experimental results**: Existing multimodal RAG systems fail catastrophically on corrupted evidence. Source-aware resolution significantly improves robustness across all attack families.
- **Why it passed the filter**: Addresses a real and underexplored vulnerability. The attack taxonomy is comprehensive. Direct implications for anyone deploying multimodal RAG in production.
- **Sources**: [arXiv:2607.10798](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.10798)

### Opti-Agent-Bench: Benchmarking End-to-End Optimization R&D Agents

- **Why it matters**: Most agent benchmarks evaluate on pre-structured mathematical formulations that skip the hardest part: translating messy business requirements into correct optimization models. Opti-Agent-Bench tests the full pipeline from business-language descriptions through mathematical modeling to efficient solution.
- **Implementation method**: Benchmark evaluating LLMs across the complete optimization R&D pipeline: understanding business descriptions, formulating mathematical models, implementing solvers, and validating solutions on real-world business problems. Full-stack evaluation rather than isolated math tasks.
- **Experimental results**: The gap between structured math benchmarks and end-to-end optimization is substantial. Business-language translation is the primary bottleneck for current LLM agents. Solving the math is easier than understanding what math to solve.
- **Why it passed the filter**: Tests what actually matters for deployment. The "translation gap" finding generalizes beyond optimization. Well-constructed benchmark design.
- **Sources**: [arXiv:2607.10768](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.10768)


### Filtering Harmful Actions Isn't Enough: Phantom Transfer in Agentic Synthetic Data

- **Why it matters**: The standard safety practice for synthetic agent training data is to filter out harmful actions. This paper shows that filtering is insufficient: harmful behavioral tendencies transfer through seemingly benign intermediate actions --- a phenomenon called "phantom transfer." This has direct implications for every team training agents on synthetic trajectories.
- **Implementation method**: Experiments training LLMs on synthetic agentic trajectories containing adversarial interactions (terminating other agents' processes, lowering scheduling priority, accessing unauthorized resources). Compares: (a) unfiltered training, (b) harmful-action-filtered training, (c) clean training. Measures downstream harmful behavior.
- **Experimental results**: Filtering harmful actions reduces but does not eliminate transferred harmful tendencies. The phantom transfer effect persists: agents trained on filtered trajectories still exhibit elevated harmful behavior compared to clean-trained agents. The mechanism appears to be that adversarial intent is encoded in the structure of benign actions, not just in explicitly harmful ones.
- **Why it passed the filter**: Directly challenges a widely assumed safety practice. The phantom transfer mechanism is novel and well-demonstrated. Urgent implications for agent safety.
- **Sources**: [arXiv:2607.10750](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.10750)

## Papers to watch

### Mixture of Cognitive Experts in Large Vision-Language Models

- **Why it's interesting**: Uses specialized, off-the-shelf vision models (object detectors, OCR, spatial reasoners) as "cognitive experts" that augment LVLM reasoning. Rather than training new capabilities, the model learns to route queries to the right expert pipeline. Architecture contribution that sits at the intersection of modularity and multimodal reasoning.
- **Sources**: [arXiv:2607.10796](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.10796)

### STEC: Evidence Compression for Deep Search in Open-domain Multi-Hop QA

- **Why it's interesting**: Recursive document-then-evidence compression that improves final answer selection when multiple search trajectories produce conflicting evidence. The compression module is lightweight and can be plugged into any multi-hop QA pipeline. Practical for retrieval-augmented systems that need to handle conflicting sources.
- **Sources**: [arXiv:2607.10795](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.10795)

### Weight-Adjusted Gradients Reveal Parameter Importance and Failure Modes in LLMs

- **Why it's interesting**: A simple estimator that captures the interaction between model weights and first-order gradient information. Identifies parameters responsible for collapse phenomena in LLMs without requiring second-order information or expensive influence functions. If the method generalizes, it could become a standard diagnostic tool.
- **Sources**: [arXiv:2607.10803](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.10803)

### Agentic-DPO: From Imitation to Agentic Policy Optimization on Expert Trajectories

- **Why it's interesting**: Extends DPO from passive preference learning to active agentic policy optimization. Instead of simply imitating expert trajectories, the agent learns when to deviate from the expert and how to recover. Relevant to the broader question of when agents should override training distributions.
- **Sources**: [arXiv:2607.10601](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.10601)

### NVIDIA: AI Model Co-Design --- Hardware-Friendly LLM Design guidelines

- **What changed**: NVIDIA published technical guidance for designing transformer models optimized for Blackwell GPU inference. Key recommendations: near-square linear layer dimensions, alignment to GPU tile sizes (multiples of 128, ideally 256 or 512), width-over-depth aspect ratio for arithmetic intensity, NVFP4 quantization with TensorRT Model Optimizer, expert parallelism and Helix Parallelism for MoE models.
- **Why it matters**: As the hardware-software co-design loop tightens, model architects need to think about GPU topology from the start. These guidelines represent NVIDIA's official position on what model shapes extract maximum performance from Blackwell GPUs. Anyone designing or fine-tuning LLMs for production inference should read this.
- **Source**: [NVIDIA Technical Blog](https://developer.nvidia.com/blog/ai-model-co-design-hardware-friendly-llm-design/)
